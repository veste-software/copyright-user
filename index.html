Guidance for

Protected Works

Copyright law protects ‘literary and artistic works’, a broad expression which includes a wide range of creative output such as books, paintings, music, film, plays, computer code, photographs, architecture, illustrations, and sculptures, among others. ‘Derivative works’ such as translations and adaptations of existing works are also protected. In the European Union, works are protected by copyright if they can be considered ‘original’. The Court of Justice of the European Union has established that a work is original if it is the ‘author’s own intellectual creation’. This means that the work must involve a sufficient level of creativity to reflect the author’s personality. More ‘entrepreneurial’ types of work such as sound recordings or broadcasts are protected by ‘related rights’, and so are performances.

    Related Cases
    Type(s) of Work
    Case
    Writing
    Infopaq International A/S v. Danske Dagblades Forening [2009] (C-5/08)

    The Court found that an 11-word extract from a newspaper article can be protected by copyright. According to the Court, parts of a copyright work can attract copyright protection so long as ‘they contain elements which are the expression of the intellectual creation of the author of the work’.

    Visual Art | Software
    Bezpečnostní softwarová asociace–Svaz softwarové ochrany v. Ministerstvo kultury (C-5/08)

    The Court indicated that a graphic user interface can be protected by copyright if the interface is ‘its author’s own intellectual creation’.

    Performance | Film
    Football Association Premier League Ltd v. QC Leisure and others and Karen Murphy v. Media Protection Services Ltd (Joined Cases) [2011](ECR I-9083) (ECJ, Grand Chamber)

    The Court stated that football matches cannot be protected by copyright, because they do not meet the originality requirement. According to the Court, ‘sporting events cannot be regarded as intellectual creations classifiable as works within the meaning of the Copyright Directive. That applies in particular to football matches, which are subject to rules of the game, leaving no room for creative freedom for the purposes of copyright.’

    Visual Art
    Eva-Maria Painer v. Standard VerlagsGmbH [2012](C-145/10) (ECJ)

    Considering a portrait photograph of a young girl, the Court indicated that a portrait photograph would be an ‘intellectual creation’ and therefore protected by copyright if the photographer had been able to stamp the work with their ‘personal touch’.

    Writing
    Football Dataco v. Yahoo! UK [2012] (C-604/10) (ECJ)

    The Court stated that “[a]s regards the setting up of a database, the criterion of originality is satisfied when, through the selection or arrangement of the data which it contains, its author expresses his creative ability in an original manner by making free and creative choices ... and thus stamps his ‘personal touch’.”

    Software Development
    SAS Institute v. World Programming [2012](C-406/10) (ECJ, Grand Chamber)

    Considering whether a user manual for a computer program may be protected by copyright, the Court stated that ‘keywords, syntax, commands and combinations of commands, options, defaults and iterations’ used in creating software are not protected because they consist of ‘words, figures or mathematical concepts.’ However, the Court recognised that the ‘choice, sequence and combination’ of these elements might be such that ‘author may express his creativity in an original manner’ and so create a protected ‘intellectual creation’.

There are various types of licences: exclusive, non-exclusive, open and collective.

If someone uses a protected work in any of the ways covered by economic or related rights without permission, and the use is not covered by exceptions, then those rights are infringed and can be enforced. Enforcement can be private (e.g. take down on YouTube) or public (going to court). Public enforcement can be civil, administrative or criminal.
